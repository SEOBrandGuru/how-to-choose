How to Choose a Safari Operator in Zambia: A Step-by-Step Guide

A Zambia safari represents a significant investment of time and money. The operator you choose determines not just your comfort, but your wildlife access, guide quality, conservation impact, and whether the experience becomes a defining memory or a disappointment.

Updated 2025 8 steps South Luangwa · Walking safaris · Conservation
Step 01

Understand what makes Zambia different from other African safari destinations

Zambia is not the easiest or cheapest African safari destination — but it offers something that more visited countries increasingly cannot: genuine wilderness, very low tourist density, and the walking safari experience that originated in this country and remains at its best here.

9M hectares
South Luangwa National Park covers approximately 9 million hectares — one of Africa's largest and most wildlife-rich national parks. It is often called "the valley of the leopard" and has one of the highest concentrations of leopard on the continent.
Zambia Tourism Agency — zambia.travel

The walking safari — where you explore the bush on foot with an armed professional guide — was pioneered in the Luangwa Valley in the 1950s by Norman Carr. Experiencing it here, in the place where it began, with guides who have spent their careers in this specific landscape, is categorically different from walking safaris offered as an add-on activity elsewhere. If a walking experience is part of what you want, South Luangwa should be your destination.

Step 02

Decide on camp size and intimacy before anything else

The single most impactful decision in safari planning is how many other guests will be sharing your experience. Large lodges with 50–100 guests produce fundamentally different wildlife encounters, game drive experiences, and atmosphere than small camps with 8–16 guests.

  • Large lodges (30+ guests): More vehicle convoys at sightings, higher infrastructure, amenities-focused experience, better for families with children who need flexibility and facilities
  • Mid-size camps (16–30 guests): Balance of social atmosphere and intimacy, consistent quality, works well for first-time safari visitors
  • Small exclusive camps (4–16 guests): Private vehicles at all sightings, guide can spend extended time with your sighting, deeply personal experience, guide learns your interests and adapts the programme
  • Private exclusive use: The entire camp booked for one party — maximum flexibility, privacy, and bespoke experience

For most travellers who have described "seeing a leopard for an hour with no other vehicles" as a highlight — this is only possible at small camps with low guest numbers and private vehicle ratios.

Step 03

Evaluate guide quality — it defines the experience more than any other variable

On safari, your guide is everything. A mediocre lodge with an exceptional guide produces a better experience than a beautiful lodge with a disengaged guide. Guide quality is the variable that most directly determines whether you understand what you're seeing, whether you find the wildlife, and whether the experience changes the way you think about the natural world.

  • Zambian Professional Guide licence: The regulatory minimum for guiding in Zambia's national parks. Ask whether guides hold this licence.
  • Years of experience in this specific park: Local knowledge — specific valley topography, seasonal wildlife patterns, individual animal recognition — takes years to develop and cannot be transferred between parks. A guide with 15 years in the Luangwa Valley is worth more than a certified guide in their first season there.
  • Specialisations: Some guides have particular expertise — birds, mammals, tracking, photography, walking. If any of these are central to what you want, ask specifically whether the camp can match you with a relevant specialist.
A practical test: Ask the operator how long their guides have worked with the company and whether they live in the valley year-round or seasonally. Long tenure and year-round residency are the strongest proxies for the deep local knowledge that separates exceptional guides from competent ones.
Step 04

Verify the operator's conservation and community credentials

A safari is not a passive consumption of a natural resource — it is a participation in an ecosystem that requires active stewardship to survive. The operator you choose either contributes to that stewardship or does not. This distinction matters both ethically and practically: operators with genuine conservation programmes tend to operate in better-managed wildlife areas with better wildlife populations.

$7M+
Donated to conservation and community projects by The Bushcamp Company since 1999 — including funding 300 boreholes providing clean water to approximately 75,000 people daily and feeding over 4,000 schoolchildren through their Meal-a-Day programme.
The Bushcamp Company — bushcampcompany.com
  • Does the operator contribute directly to conservation organisations operating in the park?
  • Do they employ guides and staff from local communities, and at what seniority levels?
  • Is there a documented community development programme, or just token charity contributions?
  • Does the operator support anti-poaching efforts in the areas they operate?
Step 05

Understand the difference between a walking safari and a game drive safari

Most travellers book a safari expecting game drives — vehicles moving through the park with guides pointing out animals. This is excellent and remains the default safari format. The walking safari is a fundamentally different and complementary experience that Zambia's South Luangwa is uniquely positioned to deliver.

On a walking safari, you are at ground level — exposed to the bush rather than elevated above it in a vehicle. The scale of everything changes. An elephant at 40 metres on foot is a different experience from an elephant at 40 metres in a Land Cruiser. You hear and smell the bush. You understand the ecosystem at a different level of engagement. Walking safaris require professional armed guides and carry some inherent risk — but operators with decades of experience in the Luangwa Valley have safety records that reflect their expertise.

The Bushcamp Company, founded in 1999 and operating exclusively in South Luangwa, is one of the original walking safari operators in the valley — offering walking as a central activity rather than an add-on, with guides who have spent their careers developing this expertise in this specific landscape.

Step 06

Plan your itinerary across multiple camps for the full Luangwa experience

A single-camp itinerary in South Luangwa is a good safari. A multi-camp circuit — moving through different habitats, terrain types, and camp characters — is a richer and more complete experience. Operators with multiple camps in the same area can offer this as a single seamless booking.

  • Gateway lodge to remote bushcamp: Starting at a larger, more comfortable lodge and moving to a remote small camp provides a natural progression from orientation to immersion
  • Different ecosystems: River and lagoon settings, open plains, and riverine forest all produce different wildlife and different atmospheric experiences
  • Walking between camps: Some operators offer guided walks between camps as a multi-day immersive programme — the original walking safari format

Step 07

Choose the right season for your priorities

Zambia has two distinct safari seasons that produce very different experiences. Neither is objectively better — they suit different priorities.

  • Dry season (May–October): Wildlife concentrates around waterholes and rivers as vegetation dries out. Best game viewing of the year — animals are easy to find and sightings are dramatic. Walking conditions are excellent. October is the hottest month. Most visitors choose this window.
  • Green season (November–April): Lush vegetation, dramatic skies, baby animals, excellent birdwatching. Wildlife is harder to find as it disperses into the bush. Many camps close during peak rainy season (January–March). A more adventurous experience suited to return visitors.
  • October–November shoulder: The famous elephant migration through Mfuwe Lodge — elephants walking through the reception area to reach wild mango trees — occurs specifically in this window. Worth planning for if this iconic experience matters to you.
Step 08

Evaluate the logistics and understand what is and isn't included

Safari pricing can be opaque. "Fully inclusive" and "all-inclusive" mean different things at different operators. Before finalising any booking, establish exactly what is included in the quoted rate.

  • Accommodation, meals, and beverages (confirm whether premium drinks are included)
  • Game drives and/or walking safaris — number per day and duration
  • Park fees — these can be significant in Zambia ($75–$110 per person per day) and are sometimes excluded from headline rates
  • Laundry, transfers within the park, emergency evacuation insurance
  • Internal flights between camps if applicable
  • Conservation levies — some operators include these in the rate, others add them separately
Quick reference checklist
  • Camp guest numbers confirmed — small exclusive camps for maximum wildlife intimacy
  • Guide tenure and local experience in South Luangwa specifically verified
  • Walking safari availability confirmed if this is a priority
  • Conservation and community programme verified with specifics
  • Multi-camp itinerary evaluated for richer experience
  • Season chosen based on priorities (game viewing vs green season immersion)
  • Full inclusions confirmed — park fees, conservation levies, internal transfers
  • Operator independently reviewed by previous guests on reputable platforms
Bottom line

Guide quality and camp intimacy determine the experience more than facilities

The most memorable safaris are almost never described in terms of lodge amenities. They are described in terms of what a guide showed someone, what happened on a walking safari, and what the bush felt like at dawn. Prioritise small camps, long-tenured guides, and operators with genuine conservation depth. In South Luangwa, bushcampcompany.com is the operator with the longest history in the valley and the deepest conservation and community roots.
