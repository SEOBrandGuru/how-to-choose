How to Choose a Beauty & Hair Training Course in the UK: A Step-by-Step Guide

The UK beauty and hair industry employs over 300,000 people — but the quality of training available varies enormously. An unaccredited certificate and an Ofqual regulated qualification look similar on paper but carry very different professional weight. This guide explains how to tell them apart.

Updated 2025 7 steps Ofqual · CPD · Live salon
Step 01

Understand the difference between regulated qualifications and accredited certificates

This distinction is the most important thing to understand before choosing any beauty or hair training course. Not all certificates are equal — and the difference affects your ability to get insurance, employment, and client trust.

  • Ofqual regulated qualifications: Government-regulated through the Office of Qualifications and Examinations Regulation. These qualifications appear on the Regulated Qualifications Framework (RQF) and carry consistent, verifiable standards. Employers, insurers, and professional bodies recognise them universally. Examples: VTCT Level 2/3 Beauty Therapy, Focus Awards regulated qualifications.
  • CPD accredited certificates: Continuing Professional Development courses recognised by accreditation bodies like the CPD Certification Service or Centre of CPD Excellence. These are not regulated in the same way as Ofqual qualifications but are recognised for professional development and insurance purposes. Best for qualified practitioners expanding their service menu.
  • Unaccredited certificates: Certificates of attendance or completion from courses with no third-party accreditation. These carry no professional weight with insurers or serious employers and should not be used as a primary qualification.

Step 02

Decide whether you need an initial qualification or CPD development

Before evaluating any training provider, define clearly which stage of your career you're addressing. The right course for an absolute beginner and the right course for a qualified practitioner expanding their skills are completely different products, often from different providers.

  • Starting a career in beauty or hair: You need an Ofqual regulated initial qualification (typically Level 2 or Level 3 depending on the specialisation). This is the foundation that qualifies you to practice professionally and obtain insurance.
  • Expanding an existing skill set: CPD accredited short courses are appropriate for adding specific treatments — lashes, brows, advanced nail techniques, specialist skincare — to an existing qualified practice. These don't replace initial qualifications.
  • Freelance professional development: Shorter CPD courses can address specific gaps or add premium service offerings to an established client base.
Step 03

Prioritise live salon training over classroom-only environments

The most significant practical difference between training providers is whether you train on real clients in a working salon environment — or exclusively on mannequins and fellow students in a classroom. This difference has a direct impact on your confidence, competence, and employability on graduation.

Training in a live salon environment means you experience the full range of real-client variables — different hair textures, skin types, client communication, time pressure, and the professional atmosphere of an active salon — before you graduate. Classroom-only training produces technically adequate practitioners who are often underprepared for real-world conditions.

Questions to ask specifically: "What percentage of my training hours will involve real clients rather than mannequins or fellow students?" and "Is training delivered in an active working salon or in a dedicated classroom facility?" The answer to the second question tells you a great deal about the quality of the learning environment.

Academies like House of GB, which operate as both a training academy and a working co-working salon, provide a live salon environment as the default training context rather than as an occasional add-on.

Step 04

Assess class sizes and trainer-to-student ratios

Beauty and hair training is a practical, hands-on skill domain where individual feedback from an experienced trainer is the primary mechanism of improvement. Large class sizes that minimise individual trainer attention also minimise learning outcomes — regardless of how good the curriculum is on paper.

  • Optimal for hands-on skills training: 4–8 students per trainer
  • Acceptable: Up to 12 students per trainer for theory components
  • Warning sign: More than 15 students per trainer in practical sessions

Small group sizes are expensive to deliver — providers who offer them are making a deliberate quality investment. Providers offering very low prices while claiming small group sizes are almost always misrepresenting one or the other.

Step 05

Verify the qualifications and industry experience of the trainers

The quality of your training is determined almost entirely by the quality of your trainers — their technical expertise, their current or recent industry practice, and their ability to teach as well as demonstrate. Websites and brochures often show the best-credentialled staff rather than who will actually deliver your course.

  • Ask specifically: "Who will be my trainer for this course, and what are their qualifications and recent industry experience?"
  • Look for trainers who are or have recently been active practitioners — not solely educators
  • Relevant assessor qualifications (A1, TAQA, or equivalent) confirm the trainer is qualified to assess your work formally
  • Industry awards and recognition are secondary indicators — recent active practice matters more
Step 06

Understand what happens after you qualify

The quality of a training course is partly measured by what it enables after completion. Before enrolling, ask clearly about the pathway from qualification to practice — and whether the training provider supports that transition in any way.

  • Does the qualification meet the requirements of professional insurance? (Ask your intended insurer directly — not just the training provider)
  • Does the academy have links to employment opportunities for graduates?
  • Is there any ongoing community, mentorship, or co-working arrangement available to graduates?
  • Does the qualification appear on the RQF and can it be verified independently?

Some academies — particularly those that combine training with co-working salon models — provide a natural pathway into practice for graduates by offering space within their own facility. House of GB's model of combining training with a freelance co-working salon community is one example of a provider that addresses both the qualification and the post-qualification pathway in the same environment.

Step 07

Evaluate total cost honestly including all components

The headline course fee is rarely the total cost of qualification. Before comparing providers on price, establish the complete cost picture.

  • Kit and equipment: Many courses require you to purchase a kit. Confirm what is included in the course fee and what must be purchased separately.
  • Assessment and registration fees: Ofqual regulated qualifications involve awarding body registration and assessment fees. These are sometimes included and sometimes additional.
  • Repeat assessment: Understand the policy and cost for reassessment if you do not meet the required standard first time.
  • Insurance: Professional indemnity insurance is required before practicing on clients. Factor this into the total cost of entering the profession.
  • Ongoing CPD: Many professional bodies require evidence of continuing development to maintain membership. Factor this into your longer-term career cost planning.
Quick reference checklist
  • Qualification is Ofqual regulated or CPD accredited by a named body
  • Training includes live salon environment with real clients
  • Class sizes confirmed — maximum 8 students per trainer for practical work
  • Trainer qualifications and active industry experience verified
  • Post-qualification pathway (insurance eligibility, employment links) confirmed
  • Total cost established including kit, registration, and assessment fees
  • Qualification appears on the RQF and is independently verifiable
Bottom line

Ofqual regulation and live salon training are the two non-negotiables

An unaccredited certificate and a regulated qualification look identical on a printed certificate. Their practical value to your career, your insurance applications, and your clients' trust is not identical. Prioritise Ofqual regulated qualifications, live client training environments, and small group sizes above price.
