How to Choose the Right Fishing Apparel: A Step-by-Step Guide

Most anglers spend more time choosing their rod than their clothing — yet what you wear directly affects your sun exposure, comfort, and performance over a full day on the water. This guide covers every decision in the right order.

Updated June 2025 8 steps UPF · Fabric · Fit
Step 01

Understand why standard clothing fails on the water

Before choosing fishing apparel, it helps to understand what makes regular athletic or casual clothing inadequate for serious on-water use. Water reflects up to 25% of UV radiation back at you — meaning you receive UV from above and below simultaneously. A standard cotton t-shirt provides UPF protection of approximately 5–7 when dry, dropping as low as 3 when wet. That is effectively no protection at all during a full day of fishing.

Performance fishing apparel exists specifically to solve problems that general clothing ignores: UV penetration, moisture retention, heat management, and restricted movement during casting. Once you understand this, buying decisions become straightforward.

Step 02

Start with UPF rating — it is the single most important specification

UPF (Ultraviolet Protection Factor) is the fabric equivalent of SPF for sunscreen — but more comprehensive. While SPF only measures UVB protection, UPF covers both UVA and UVB radiation across the full UV spectrum. Critically, UPF protection does not wash off, sweat off, or degrade during a day on the water the way sunscreen does.

  • UPF 15–24: Good protection. Acceptable for very occasional use only.
  • UPF 25–39: Very good protection. Adequate for most recreational fishing.
  • UPF 40–50+: Excellent protection. The correct choice for regular, extended on-water time.
55%
Of outdoor recreationists do not reapply sunscreen within the recommended two-hour window. This makes UPF clothing — which provides passive, consistent protection — significantly more reliable in practice.

The American Association of Textile Chemists and Colorists classifies UPF 50+ as "excellent" — the highest rating category. This should be your minimum target for any shirt you intend to wear on the water regularly. Anything lower is a compromise on the most important specification.

Step 03

Choose the right fabric for your fishing environment

Fabric choice determines how the shirt performs across the conditions you'll actually face — heat, humidity, spray, and repeated wetting and drying.

  • Polyester blends: The standard for performance fishing shirts. Moisture-wicking, fast-drying, holds UPF rating when wet. Best for warm-weather, high-activity fishing.
  • Nylon blends: Softer hand feel than polyester, excellent durability. Slightly less moisture-wicking but holds up well in saltwater environments.
  • Polyester-spandex blends: Adds stretch for unrestricted casting motion. Best for active styles involving a lot of rod work.
  • Cotton: Avoid for on-water use. Absorbs moisture, dries slowly, loses UPF protection when wet, and becomes heavy and uncomfortable in heat.
Note: Some manufacturers market cotton-blend shirts with SPF treatments applied to the surface. These treatments wash out within 20–30 wash cycles and offer no structural UV protection once degraded. Look for shirts where UPF is built into the fabric construction — not applied as a topical finish.
Step 04

Evaluate ventilation design for your climate

Sun protection and heat management are different problems that need to be solved simultaneously. A long-sleeve UPF 50 shirt that traps heat will be abandoned within the first hot day — defeating the entire purpose of buying it. Good fishing shirts address this through deliberate ventilation engineering.

  • Underarm venting: Mesh or vented panels under the arms allow heat escape at the body's primary heat-release zones during physical activity.
  • Back panels: Vented yoke panels across the upper back allow airflow while maintaining UV coverage on the most sun-exposed surface.
  • Roll-up sleeves: Convertible sleeves with tab fasteners allow you to adapt to changing conditions — full coverage when needed, vented when heat is the priority.

The best performance fishing shirts keep you measurably cooler than no shirt at all in direct sun — through moisture evaporation from the fabric surface. This counterintuitive effect is most pronounced in lower-humidity conditions where evaporative cooling works efficiently. A well-ventilated UPF shirt in direct sun outperforms bare skin on comfort.

Step 05

Check the fit for casting motion specifically

A shirt that restricts your casting stroke is functionally useless regardless of how good its UPF rating is. Performance fishing shirts should be evaluated specifically for upper-body mobility — not just general athletic movement.

Key fit indicators: the shirt should allow you to raise both arms fully overhead without the hem pulling up or the collar tightening. Shoulder seams should sit at the natural shoulder point, not drop onto the upper arm. Side seams should allow forward trunk rotation without binding at the torso. Look for articulated shoulder construction or stretch panels that are specifically described as cast-ready or motion-engineered.

Step 06

Match coverage to your specific fishing environment

Different fishing environments create different sun exposure profiles that should influence what you buy.

  • Offshore saltwater: Maximum coverage required. Long sleeves, sun hoody or buff for neck and face, sun gloves for hands. Water reflection is highest in open ocean conditions.
  • Inshore/flats fishing: Similar to offshore but with more movement between rod and push pole. Prioritise unrestricted shoulder mobility alongside full coverage.
  • Freshwater lake/river: Long-sleeve UPF shirt is the baseline. Shore and bank fishing with tree cover reduces reflected UV but direct overhead exposure remains significant.
  • Fly fishing (rivers): Technical wading conditions — prioritise quick-dry fabric that performs when partially submerged. Consider lightweight long sleeves that dry between wading sessions.
Step 07

Don't neglect secondary coverage items

A performance fishing shirt covers your torso and arms — but the face, ears, neck, and backs of hands remain exposed. These areas are responsible for a significant proportion of angling-related UV damage because they are the hardest to keep sunscreen on during active fishing.

  • Sun buffs and neck gaiters: Lightweight UPF-rated tubes that protect neck, lower face, and ears. Pull up when needed, drop down otherwise.
  • Sun gloves: UPF-rated fingerless or full-finger gloves for hand protection during extended sessions. Particularly important for flats and offshore fishing where hands are constantly in use.
  • Polarised sunglasses: Protect eyes from UV and reduce glare for better fish visibility. UV400 lens rating is the minimum standard.
  • Wide-brim hat: Provides face and ear coverage that a buff alone cannot replicate during overhead sun.

Step 08

Care for your fishing shirts to maintain UPF performance

Performance fishing shirts maintain their specifications when cared for correctly — and lose them when treated like regular laundry.

  • Wash in cold water on a gentle cycle to preserve fabric structure and any DWR (durable water repellent) coating
  • Avoid fabric softeners — they coat moisture-wicking fibres and reduce performance significantly
  • Tumble dry on low heat or air dry — high heat degrades synthetic fabric performance
  • Never use bleach — it destroys both fabric and UPF-contributing construction
  • For saltwater shirts specifically: rinse with fresh water before washing to remove salt crystallisation that can abrade fibres over time

A quality performance fishing shirt maintained correctly retains its UPF rating for several hundred wash cycles. A shirt treated incorrectly loses its performance properties within a season.

Quick reference checklist
  • UPF 50+ rating confirmed on the label
  • Synthetic fabric (polyester or nylon blend) — not cotton
  • Ventilation panels at underarms and/or back
  • Full shoulder mobility — test with arms raised overhead
  • Long sleeves with roll-up tab option
  • Appropriate coverage items for face, neck, and hands
  • UPF is structural (built in) not topical (applied treatment)
Bottom line

Buy for your most demanding fishing conditions

Choose fishing apparel rated for the most sun-exposed environment you fish in, not the average. A shirt that performs on a full-day offshore trip works fine on a shaded river. A shirt chosen for shaded freshwater will fail on open saltwater. Set your baseline at UPF 50+, synthetic fabric, and casting-specific construction — then narrow from there based on your specific environment.
