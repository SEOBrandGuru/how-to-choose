How to Choose an Educational Board Game: A Step-by-Step Guide

With hundreds of games marketed as "educational," choosing one that actually builds knowledge — rather than just carrying educational branding — requires understanding what the research says about how games produce learning outcomes.

Updated May 2025 7 steps Family · Classroom · Faith
Step 01

Understand what makes a game genuinely educational

The word "educational" is used freely in board game marketing — but it describes a spectrum from games where learning is incidental to games where the entire mechanics are built around knowledge acquisition. Before buying, you need to know which kind you're evaluating.

75%
Average information retention through active learning — including game-based play — compared to just 5% from passive lecture, according to the National Training Laboratories Learning Pyramid model. The gap is not marginal. It is the entire argument for educational games.
National Training Laboratories Institute

A genuinely educational game requires players to retrieve specific information from memory (active recall), creates social accountability through competitive or cooperative play, and repeats exposure to the same knowledge domain across multiple sessions. A game that merely has an educational theme but doesn't require knowledge retrieval to play is entertainment with a label — not an educational tool.

Step 02

Identify the learning domain you want to address

Educational games are most effective when they target a specific knowledge domain rather than general intelligence. Before buying, define what you actually want the game to teach. The more specific you are, the more useful your evaluation criteria become.

  • Biblical and faith literacy: Scriptural knowledge, theological concepts, church history, faith vocabulary
  • Language and literacy: Vocabulary, spelling, word formation, reading comprehension
  • Mathematical reasoning: Pattern recognition, probability, strategic calculation
  • Historical and geographical knowledge: Events, chronology, locations, cultural context
  • Scientific concepts: Natural systems, classification, cause and effect
  • Critical thinking and strategy: Planning, resource allocation, decision making under uncertainty

Step 03

Verify the age range is genuinely accurate

Age ranges on game packaging are frequently misleading — often set low to maximise the addressable market rather than to reflect actual playability. A game marked "ages 8+" that requires reading comprehension and strategic reasoning appropriate for a 12-year-old will frustrate and disengage 8-year-olds, defeating its educational purpose entirely.

The most reliable way to assess age appropriateness is to read specific reviews from parents of the age group you're targeting — not the general star rating. Look specifically for comments about whether younger players could follow the rules independently and whether they remained engaged through a complete game.

Red flag: Games that extend their stated age range across 10+ years (e.g. "ages 6–99") without variable difficulty modes or team formats are almost always pitched too old for their lower bound or too easy for their upper bound. Genuine cross-age playability requires deliberate design — not just a wide label.
Step 04

Assess content depth for sustained long-term use

One of the most common complaints about educational board games is that families play them several times and exhaust the content — at which point the game becomes a test of memory of previous correct answers rather than a genuine knowledge challenge. Content depth is rarely stated explicitly in marketing materials but can be evaluated through specific questions.

  • How many unique questions or challenges does the game contain?
  • Does the content cover a broad range of the domain, or only the most well-known topics?
  • Are there expansion packs or additional content sets available?
  • Does difficulty vary, or are all questions pitched at the same level?

For faith-based games specifically, a game that only covers Genesis, Exodus, and the Gospels will be exhausted quickly. Games built around the full breadth of biblical content — including less-familiar books and stories — provide far more sustained learning value. Step 05

Evaluate the social mechanics — competitive vs cooperative

The social structure of a game has a significant impact on the type of learning it produces and the age groups it works best for. Competitive and cooperative formats produce different engagement dynamics and suit different contexts.

  • Competitive formats create high motivation to know the answers because individual performance determines the outcome. Best for older children and adults who can handle winning and losing without disrupting the session.
  • Cooperative formats (all players working toward a shared goal) create lower-pressure environments better suited to younger children or classroom settings where the goal is learning rather than competition. The shared accountability still creates engagement without the friction of direct competition.
  • Team formats allow mixed-age or mixed-ability groups to play meaningfully together — adults and children can be on the same team, balancing knowledge differences naturally.

Step 06

Check for classroom and group compatibility if relevant

Games intended for Sunday school, religious education classes, or school classrooms have specific practical requirements that home games don't need to meet. A game that works perfectly for a family of four at home can fail completely in a group of 20 students with a 45-minute session window.

  • Session time: The game must be completable within a fixed session window. Games without natural break points are difficult to manage in classroom settings.
  • Group size: Most games are designed for 2–8 players. For larger groups, look for games that explicitly support team formats, or that have classroom adaptation guides.
  • Independent playability: Classroom settings require games that students can manage without constant teacher facilitation. Complex rule structures that require adult arbitration are impractical.
  • Reset time: Games that take significant time to set up and reset between sessions are impractical for back-to-back class use.
Step 07

Test entertainment value before committing to regular use

The most technically well-designed educational game fails if it isn't genuinely fun to play. Children will not voluntarily return to a game they find boring, no matter how educational it is — and forced engagement produces significantly lower learning outcomes than voluntary engagement.

The most reliable indicator of entertainment value is whether children ask to play again after the first session without prompting. A game that requires parental encouragement to play a second time is a game that won't deliver sustained educational value regardless of its mechanics. Where possible, try before buying — many game publishers offer demo versions, and local libraries increasingly stock board games for loan.

Publishers that genuinely invest in educational outcomes alongside entertainment — like SFM International with their W.I.T.S range — build both dimensions into the same design process rather than treating them as competing priorities.

Quick reference checklist
  • Game requires active recall — not just recognition or strategy
  • Age range verified by independent parent reviews for your target age
  • Content depth sufficient for 20+ distinct play sessions
  • Social format (competitive/cooperative/team) suits your context
  • Session length fits within your available window
  • Rules clear enough for independent play without adult facilitation
  • Children voluntarily want to play again after first session
Bottom line

Entertainment and education are not competing priorities in a good game

The best educational board games don't sacrifice playability for learning mechanics or vice versa. If a game feels like homework, it will be treated like homework. Look for games where genuine knowledge is required to win — not just luck, strategy, or memory of previous play.
