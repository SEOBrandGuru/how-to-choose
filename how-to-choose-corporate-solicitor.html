How to Choose a Corporate Solicitor in Manchester: A Step-by-Step Guide

41% of all legal complaints received by the Legal Ombudsman relate to costs — most of which could have been avoided with the right questions asked before instruction. This guide walks through every step in the correct order.

Updated April 2025 8 steps Corporate · Commercial · Manchester
Step 01

Define exactly what type of legal help you need before searching

The term "corporate solicitor" covers a wide range of distinct practice areas. Instructing a firm strong in M&A when you need employment advice — or a general commercial firm when you need IP protection — wastes time and often produces poor outcomes. Before contacting any firm, define your need specifically.

  • Business formation and structuring: Company setup, shareholder agreements, articles of association
  • Commercial contracts: Drafting, reviewing, and negotiating supplier, customer, and partnership agreements
  • Employment law: Contracts of employment, disciplinary procedures, tribunal defence, redundancy
  • Corporate transactions: Mergers, acquisitions, investment rounds, management buyouts
  • Dispute resolution: Commercial litigation, mediation, arbitration
  • Intellectual property: Trademark registration, IP protection, licensing agreements
  • Regulatory compliance: Industry-specific regulatory frameworks and ongoing compliance advice

The more precisely you define your need, the better placed you are to evaluate whether a specific firm or solicitor actually has the depth of experience your matter requires.

Step 02

Verify SRA registration — this is non-negotiable

Every practising solicitor in England and Wales must be registered with the Solicitors Regulation Authority. This is not a formality — it is the mechanism that ensures your solicitor is insured, accountable, and subject to professional conduct rules. Unregistered practitioners offer no such protections.

140,000
Practising solicitors registered with the SRA in England and Wales. All are searchable on the SRA's free public register at sra.org.uk — checking takes under 60 seconds and should be done before any other evaluation step.
Solicitors Regulation Authority — sra.org.uk

Go to sra.org.uk/consumers/register, search the firm and individual solicitor's name, and confirm both are currently authorised. Do this before calling, before meetings, and before signing anything.

Step 03

Prioritise local Manchester expertise over national name recognition

For most business legal matters in Greater Manchester, a firm with deep local roots outperforms a national firm with a Manchester office on several dimensions that directly affect your outcome and experience.

  • Face-to-face meetings are available at short notice when matters become urgent

Step 04

Ask the right questions about relevant experience

General firm reputation and award listings tell you very little about whether this specific solicitor has handled matters like yours recently and successfully. Ask directly and specifically.

  • "How many matters in this practice area have you handled in the last 12 months?"
  • "What is the typical size and type of business you advise in this area?"
  • "Who will handle my matter day-to-day — a partner, an associate, or a trainee?"
  • "Can you give me an example (anonymised) of a similar matter you've handled and how it concluded?"
  • "Do you have specific experience in my industry sector?"
Watch out for: Firms that answer experience questions with generalities ("we have an excellent team") rather than specifics. A solicitor who can't give you numbers or examples for their claimed practice area probably doesn't handle it as regularly as their website suggests.
Step 05

Understand every element of the fee structure before agreeing to anything

The single most common source of client dissatisfaction with solicitors is unexpected cost. This is almost always preventable with the right conversation before instruction.

41%
Of all complaints received by the Legal Ombudsman relate to costs — either unexpected charges, poor initial cost information, or final bills significantly higher than estimates. This is the most common complaint category by a significant margin.
Legal Ombudsman Annual Report — legalombudsman.org.uk
  • Hourly rate billing: You pay for time spent. Requires active management — ask for a cost estimate and agree a budget ceiling before work begins.
  • Fixed fees: Available for defined tasks (company formation, standard contract drafts). Provides certainty but only applies to the specific scope agreed.
  • Retainer / Legal-as-a-Service: Monthly subscription for ongoing access to legal advice. Predictable cost, appropriate for businesses with regular legal needs.
  • Conditional fee (no win no fee): Common in litigation. Understand exactly what percentage is taken and under what conditions.

Whatever structure is agreed, request a written cost estimate covering the full scope of the matter — and ask specifically what additional costs might arise outside that scope. Disbursements (court fees, barrister fees, search fees) are frequently not included in initial estimates.

Step 06

Read the client care letter carefully before signing

The client care letter is the document your solicitor must provide before substantive work begins — required by SRA rules. It defines the scope of work, fee arrangement, who handles your matter, and the complaints procedure. Most clients sign it without reading it carefully. This is a significant mistake.

Specifically check: that the scope of work matches exactly what you discussed verbally; that the fee arrangement reflects what was agreed; that the named supervising partner is who you expect; and that the complaints procedure is clearly stated. If anything in the letter contradicts your understanding of the agreement, do not sign until it is resolved in writing.

Step 07

Assess communication standards before the relationship begins

68% of client dissatisfaction with solicitors is attributed to communication failures — not poor legal outcomes. The quality of a firm's communication can be assessed before you instruct them.

68%
Of clients who reported dissatisfaction with their solicitor cited communication delays and lack of availability as the primary complaint — above issues with the quality of legal strategy or the outcome of their matter.
Legal Services Consumer Panel
  • How quickly does the firm respond to your initial enquiry? Slow response at the sales stage predicts slow response during the matter.
  • Who is your named point of contact for day-to-day queries?
  • What is the stated response time commitment for client communications?
  • Is there a backup contact when your primary solicitor is unavailable?
Step 08

Consider whether you need ongoing support or one-off advice

Many businesses instruct solicitors reactively — calling when something goes wrong — and pay hourly rates for work that could have been prevented with proactive advice. If your business has regular legal needs (employment decisions, contract reviews, compliance questions), a retainer arrangement typically delivers better value and better outcomes than ad hoc instruction.

Legal-as-a-Service models — where a firm provides embedded ongoing legal support at a fixed monthly cost — are increasingly offered by progressive Manchester firms and are particularly well-suited to growing SMEs that need regular access to legal expertise without the overhead of in-house counsel. Quick reference checklist

  • SRA registration verified for firm and individual solicitor
